Comparison-based and detection-based 
approaches to transfer research
ScottJarvis
OhioUniversity
ThispaperoffersanumberofrefinementstoJarvis’s(2000)methodological
frameworkforinvestigatingcross-linguisticeffects.Accordingtotheoriginal
framework,therearethreepotentialconsequencesofcross-linguisticeffects,
andanycompellingargumentfororagainstthepresenceofsucheffectsmustbe
basedonaconsiderationofallthreeconsequences.Theseconsequencescanbe
thoughtofastypesofevidence,orpremisesfortransfer,andtheirinvestigation
requiresperformancecomparisonsbetweenindividuals,groups,andlanguages.
Thepresentpaperhastwopurposes.Thefirstistocharacterizethefoundations
ofaclassificationschemethathighlightstherelationshipsamongthesethree
typesofevidenceandalsoindicatesthatthereisyetafourthtypeofevidence
forcross-linguisticeffectsthathasnotyetbeentakenaccountofwithinthe
framework.Thesecondpurposeofthispaperistoshowthatthesefourtypesof
evidenceconstitutewhatcanbedescribedasacomparison-basedargumentfor
transfer,butthattherealsoexistothervalidargumentsfortransfer,suchaswhat
Irefertoasthedetection-basedargument.Idescribeboththecomparison-and
detection-basedargumentsandthetypesofevidencetheyentailinrelationto
numberofrecentinvestigationsintocross-linguisticinfluence.
Introduction
Oneofmygoalsinthispaperistoillustratethatinstancesoftransfer,orcross-
linguisticinfluence(CLI),canbebothremarkablysimpleandatthesametime
extraordinarilycomplex.Whatdetermineswhethertheyaresimpleorcomplexis
howtheyareviewedandthedepthtowhichtheyareinvestigatedandanalysed.
ThetopicofthispaperistheidentificationandverificationofCLIeffects,andIam
particularlyinterestedinthosecasesoftransferthattendtogounnoticedbecause
theyareeithersubtleorunobservablewithouttheuseofproperanalyticaltools.
Itiscaseslikethesewheremethodologicalrigourisneededintheidentification

InanarticleIpublishedin2000(Jarvis2000),andthenagaininthebook
Irecentlyco-authoredwithAnetaPavlenko(JarvisandPavlenko2008),Ipro-
posedamethodologicalframeworkforachievingempiricalrigourintheidenti-
ficationofinstancesofCLI.Theframeworkconsistsofthreecomponents:(a)a
theory-neutraldefinition thatdefinesCLIas the relationshipbetweensource-
languagegroupmembershipandtarget-languagebehaviour,(b)aspecification
ofthetypesofevidenceforCLIthatthedefinitionimplies,and(c)alistofthe
varioustypesoffactorsthatneedtobetakenintoaccountinaninvestigationof
CLIbecauseoftheirpotentialtoproduceeffectsresemblingCLIand,conversely,
becauseoftheirpotential toobscuretheeffectsthatCLIitselfproduces.Most
of theattentiontothis frameworksofarhasbeendirectedtowardthesecond
component–thetypesofevidenceneededtoestablishthepresenceofCLIef-
fects.Allthreetypesofevidencearederivedthroughvarioustypesofcompari-
son,includingwithin-groupcomparisonsofpeoplefromthesamesource-lan-
guagebackgroundusingthesametargetlanguage,between-groupcomparisons
involving people from different source-language backgrounds using the same
target language, and cross-language comparisons involving people using both
thesourceandtargetlanguages.Iwilldescribethesetypesofevidenceinmore
detailshortly,butbeforedoingso,Iwouldliketostepbackbrieflyandtakea
somewhatbroaderperspectiveofthetypesofevidenceuponwhichanargument
fortransfercanbebuilt.
Weknowthatcomparisonsarefundamentaltotheinvestigationoftransfer,
buttheremayalsobeotherclassesofevidenceonwhichacompellingargument
fortransfercanbebuilt.Toclarifythispoint,itisusefultodrawfromthetheory
of reasoning,or logic,whichdefinesargumentsas combinationsof statements
thatincludepremisesandaconclusion.Premisescangenerallybethoughtofas
claims,andthestrengthofanargumentdependsfundamentallyonthelikelihood
ofthepremisesbeingtrue,aswellasonthedegreetowhichthetruthoftheprem-
isesentailsthetruthoftheconclusion(foranin-depthdiscussionofargument
structureandvalidity,see,e.g.,Gamut1991).Thelikelihoodofthepremisesbeing
true isstrengthenedbyevidence,and inthemethodological frameworkIhave
proposed,thethreetypesofevidencecanbethoughtofasthreepremisesresting
onthreeseparatetypesofcomparison,butwhichworktogetherincontributing
toasingleargumentaboutthepresenceorabsenceofCLIeffectsinaparticular
sampleoflanguageuse.Thatargumentisacomparison-basedargument,andone
questionthathasnotreceivedexplicitattentioninthetransferliteratureiswheth-
ertheremayalsobealternative,non-comparison-basedargumentsthatcouldbe
putforwardforthepresenceorabsenceofCLI.
 Comparison-basedanddetection-basedapproaches 11
Knowingwheretobeginthesearchforalternativeargumentsfortransferis
difficult,butitmaybehelpfultobeginwiththetypesofargumentsthatareused
inotherdomains,suchascourtsoflaw.Thetypesofargumentsthatareusedto
establishinnocenceorguiltinacriminaltrialofteninvolvecriminalconfessions,
motives, eyewitness testimony, character-witness testimony, prior convictions
andpriordocumentedbehaviouraltendencies,profilingthedefendant’scharac-
terandbehaviouraltraitsinrelationtoknowncriminalswhohavecommittedthe
sametypeofcrime,andidentificationofthecriminalonthebasisoffingerprints
and/orDNA.Wemightbeabletofindatleastindirectanalogiestosomeofthese
typesofevidence–andthetypesofargumentstheycontributeto–intransfer
research.Forexample,eyewitnesstestimonymightcorrespondinsomewaysto
learners’ownaccountsofhowtheirknowledgeofonelanguagehasaffectedtheir
useofanother,andinfactaPhDstudentinChina(whosenameIunfortunately
do not recall) recently suggested to me that introspection is a relevant type of
evidence for transfer that seems tobemissing frommy framework.Theprob-
lemwithanintrospection-(orretrospection-)basedargument,though,isthata
single,subjectivewitnessforaneventisnotverycompellingevidence.Tomake
itmoreconvincing,wecouldofcourseseewhethermultiple learnersfromthe
samelanguagebackgroundreportexperiencingsimilarCLIphenomenaintheir
languageuse,andwhetherlearnersfromdifferentlanguagebackgroundsreport
differenttypesofCLIeffects.Doingso,however,wouldbringusrightbacktothe
comparison-basedargument.
Clearly,agooddealofworkstillneedstobedonetosortoutwhattherelevant
typesofargumentsfortransferare,whattypesofevidencetheyreston,howstrong
thoseargumentsare inrelationtooneanother,andwhethertheymeetminimal
criteriaforempiricalrigour.Inthemeantime,Iwillattempttoshowthatthereisat
leastonecompellingalternativetothecomparison-basedargumentthathasbegun
toemergeinthefieldofappliedlinguistics.Irefertoitasthedetection-basedargu-
ment,andinsomewaysitisanalogoustoargumentsusedinacourtoflawthatare
basedoncriminalprofiling,fingerprints,andDNA.Thedetection-basedargument
itselfrestsontheaccuracywithwhichlearners’source-languagebackgrounds(i.e.,
theirsource-languageprofiles)canbedetected fromtheirpatternsof target-lan-
guageuse(i.e.,the“fingerprints”oftheirlanguageuse).Thisisatypeofevidence
thatmayalreadybefamiliartomanylanguageteacherswhosestudentscomefrom
avarietyofnative-languagebackgrounds.There isanecdotalevidence that,after
sufficient experience, teachers canoften takeanessaywrittenbyananonymous
languagelearnerandidentifyinstantlywhatthatlanguagelearner’snativelanguage
is,eveniftheyarenotsurewhatcluesintheessayallowedthemtomakethatdeter-
mination.Similarly,computersoftwarecanbeprogrammedtolookforanddetect
patternsoflanguageusethatareassociatedwithspecificlanguagebackgrounds.

Whentheaccuracywithwhichahumanormachineisabletoclassifysamples
oflearnerlanguagebyL1backgroundsignificantlyexceedsthelevelofchance,it
wouldbedifficulttoarguethattransfer isnotatplay,regardlessofwhetherthe
preciselinguisticmanifestationsofthattransferhavebeenfullydiscerned.Iwill
returntothispointlaterinthepaper.Thepointfornowisthatmyoriginalmeth-
odologicalframeworkdealtwithtypesofevidencethatarerelevanttoonlyasingle
argument(i.e.,thecomparison-basedargument),butthereexistpotentiallymany
typesofargumentsfortransfer,eachwithitsownsetofpremises.Thepurposeof
thispaperistocharacterizethecomparison-basedanddetection-basedarguments
andtodiscussandillustratethetypesofpremisestheyarebasedon.Thisentails
someimportantrefinementstomyoriginalframework,includingtherecognition
ofapreviouslyoverlookedfourthtypeofcomparison-basedevidencefortransfer.
The comparison-based argument
DiscussionsofthetypesofevidenceneededtoidentifyandverifyCLIeffectstendto
dealeitherwiththetypesofdatathataremostrelevantorthetypesofcomparisons
thatshouldbemadewiththosedata.Concerningtheformer,ParadisandGenesee
(1996)describedandinvestigatedthefollowingthreemanifestationsofCLI:delay,
acceleration,andtransfer.Thefirsttwoofthesemanifestationsrefertothespeed
andtimingwithwhichbilingualsareabletoacquireoneortheotheroftheirlan-
guages,andisreminiscentofwhatRingbom(1987)referstoascoverttransfer.The
thirdmanifestationofCLIisanalogoustowhatRingbomcallsoverttransfer,and
thisinvolvescaseswherebilingualsorsecondlanguagelearnerscarryoversome
oftheforms,features,functions,meanings,ordistributionalpropertiesfromone
languagewhileusingtheother.Atamoreabstractlevel,ParadisandGeneseecan
beinterpretedasarguingfortheneedforbothquantitative(i.e.,delayandaccelera-
tion)andqualitative(i.e.,transfer)evidenceforCLI.(Thiswaspointedoutbyan
anonymousreviewerofamanuscriptIrecentlyservedasactioneditorfor.)
Discussionsoftransferthatfocusonthetypesofcomparisonthatareneeded
canbefoundintheworkofGranger(1996),Jarvis(2000),Odlin(1989),Selinker
(1983, 1992), and many others. The methodological framework I proposed in
2000 isgenerally seenasencapsulating theproposalsofferedbyother scholars
andasestablishingrelativelyclearguidelinesfordeterminingrigorouslywhether
CLIeffectsarepresentinaparticularsampleoflanguageuse.Asmentionedearli-
er,theframeworkconsistsofthreegeneralcomponents,andmostoftheattention
hasbeendirectedatthecomponentthatdealswiththetypesofevidencethatare
neededtoarguefororagainstthepresenceoftransfer.Thethreetypesofevidence
arewhatIhavereferredtoasintragrouphomogeneity,intergroupheterogeneity,

and cross-language congruity (see Jarvis 2000, Jarvis and Pavlenko 2008). The
firsttwotypesofevidencerelatetowhetheranobservedpatternofperformance
inthetargetlanguageisindicativeofmembershipinaparticulargroup,suchasa
particularL1group,andthethirdtypeofevidencerelatestowhetherthatpattern
ofperformanceisalsoindicativeofaparticularsourcelanguage,suchastheL1.
Allthreetypesofevidenceinvolveaninterplayofquantitativeandqualita-
tiveconsiderations.Thefirsttwotypesofevidencetendtobeusedwithaquan-
titativeemphasisinshowing,forexample,howcommonaparticularpatternof
target-languageuse isamong learners fromaparticularsource-languageback-
ground(i.e., intragrouphomogeneity;see,e.g.,Jarvis2000,Selinker1983),and
inestablishingwhetherlearnersfromdifferentsource-languagebackgroundsuse
thatpatternwithsignificantlydifferentratesofoccurrence(i.e.,intergrouphet-
erogeneity;see,e.g.,Olshtain1983,Ringbom1987).Thethirdtypeofevidence,in
turn,isoftenusedwithaqualitativeemphasisbyshowingthatlearners’(orbilin-
guals’)patternsofperformanceinthesourceandtargetlanguagearequalitatively
similar(i.e.,cross-languagecongruity;see,e.g.,ParadisandGenesee1996,Beebe,
TakahashiandUliss-Weltz1990).However,quantitativeandqualitativeevidence
areoftentwosidesofthesamecoininthesensethatthewaywequantifydatais
determinedbyqualitativecriteria,andwhatweevaluatequalitativelycannearly
alwaysbequantifiedintermsofmagnitudeofsimilarityand/orfrequencyofoc-
currence.Evidenceforintragrouphomogeneity,forexample,canbegatheredei-
therwithrespect to theproportionofagroup that showsaparticularpattern,
orwithrespecttothetypesofsimilaritiesthatarefoundamongtheindividuals
withinthegroup,orboth.Theothertwotypesofevidencecanlikewiseemphasize
eitherquantitativeorqualitativerelationships,orboth.
Asmentionedearlier,itisusefultothinkofthesethreetypesofevidenceas
threepremisesofasingleargument.Importantly,eachofthepremisesbyitself
iscompatiblewithmultipleconclusions(or interpretations),someofwhichdo
notinvolveCLI.Thecombinationofallthreepremises,ontheotherhand,points
moreorlessexclusivelytoaconclusionofCLIeffects,dependingonhowstrong
the evidence for each premise is. Because each premise by itself is compatible
withmultipleconclusions,Ioriginallysuggestedthatanyrigorousinvestigation
ofCLIwillsearchforallthreetypesofevidence,andwillnotmakeclaimsabout
transferwithouthavingfoundatleasttwoofthem(seeJarvis2000).Odlin(2003)
laterclaimedthatsomecasesoftransferaresoobviousthattheydonotrequirean
examinationofallthreetypesofevidence.Inmyownexperienceasaresearcher,
itseemsthatallthreetypesofevidencearealwaysrelevant,butitissometimes
sufficienttodrawoneormoretypesofevidencefromothersources,suchasthe
resultsofpriorstudies,publishedgrammarsandlanguagehistories,andpersonal
experiences(seeJarvisandPavlenko2008:48–51).

framework.Theseincludetheadditionofafourthtypeofevidencederivedfrom
comparisonsbetweenlearnersandnativespeakers,orafourthtypeofevidence
derivedfromcomparisonsofstructuraloccurrencefrequencies inthe learners’
L1andtheiruseofthetargetlanguage(e.g.,Paquot2007).Iseethesetwotypes
ofevidenceasalreadybeingsubsumedinmymethodologicalframeworkunder
intergroupheterogeneityandcross-languagecongruity,respectively,butthesug-

listratherthanasataxonomyorclassificationschemethatwouldindicateexactly

eters.Tosolvethisproblem,Ihaveproceededsimultaneouslyalongthefollowing
twoavenues:(a)developingaclassificationschemeforCLIevidencetypesand(b)

mightnotalreadybetakenaccountofbytheoriginalframework.Bothavenues
havebeenfruitful,andbothhaveledtothesamesolution.Idescribebothavenues

The literature does indeed contain many examples of comparison-based
evidence for transfer that isnot taken intoaccount inmyoriginal framework,
andinmostifnotallcases,thatevidencedoesinfactseemtoinvolveintralin-
gualcontrasts.IfirstbecameawareofthisfactwhenreadingParibakht(2005)a
fewyearsago,anditbecameevenclearerwhenIlaterdirectedanMAthesisby
Shehata(2008).ThestudybyParibakht(2005)dividedtargetwords inEnglish
intothosethathavecounterpartsinFarsiandthosethatdonot,andtheauthor’s
evidenceforCLIeffects isthatFarsi-speakinglearnersofEnglisharefarbetter
atinferringthemeaningsofEnglishwordsthatarealsolexicalizedinFarsithan
theyareatinferringEnglishwordmeaningsthatarenotlexicalizedintheirL1.
The thesis by Shehata (2008), in turn, divided English collocations into those
thatarecongruentwithArabiccollocationsandthosethatareincongruent.She
foundthatArabic-speakinglearnersofEnglishshowhigherlevelsofaccuracyin
theirknowledgeanduseofEnglishcollocationsthatarecongruentwithArabic
(seeYamashita2009forasimilarstudyinvolvingJapanese-speakinglearnersof
English).Althoughtheresultsofthesestudiesarenotparticularlysurprising,the
comparisonstheyreliedonclearly illustratetheutilityof intralingualcontrasts
andtheirstrengthasevidenceforCLI.
Evidencefortransferinvolvingintralingualcontrastsisnotlimitedtobinary
comparisonsbetweencongruentandincongruentstructures,however.Structures
inthetargetlanguagecanvaryalongawideandcomplex(possiblymultidimen-
sional)continuumwithrespecttohowcongruentorincongruenttheyarewith
structuresofthesourcelanguage,andanumberofrecentstudieshavetakenthis
factintoaccount.Inonesuchstudy,TokowiczandMacWhinney(2005)exam-
inedintralingualcontrastsalongasimilaritycontinuuminvolvingstructuresthat
are similar (i.e., tense markers) and different (determiner number agreement)
betweenL1EnglishandL2Spanish,aswellasstructuresthatareuniquetothe
L2(determinergenderagreement).(ThiscontinuumresemblesRingbom’s2007
distinctionbetweensimilarities,differences,andzerorelationships.)Thepartici-
pants, who were university-level English-speaking learners of Spanish with no
morethantwoyearsofL2instruction,weregivenanL2grammaticalityjudgment
taskinvolvingallthreetypesofstructures.Theresearchersexaminedthelearners’
performanceonthistaskinrelationtotheirresponseaccuracyandtheirelectro-
physiologicalbrainresponses(event-relatedpotentials,orERPs)togrammatical
violationsversuscorrectstructures.Eventhoughthelearners’responseaccuracy

InastudyofSwedish-speaking learnersofGerman,BohnackerandRosén
(2008)examinedevenmoresubtleandintricateintralingualcontrasts.Swedish
andGermanarebothverb-second(V2)languages,whichmeansthattheyrequire
thefiniteverbinadeclarativeindependentclausetobethesecondgrammatical
constituent. Incaseswhereanobjectoradverbial is fronted(i.e., to thebegin-
ningof theclause), the clauseundergoes subject-verb inversion,with theverb
remaining in second position, and the subject moving to third position. Even
thoughbothlanguagesaresubjecttothesameV2grammaticalityconstraint,they
differinsubtlewaysintermsofwhattypeofinformationcanconventionallybe
placedatthebeginningofthesentence.UsingnativeGermanandnativeSwedish
corpora,theresearchersshowedthatSwedishhasagreaterpreferenceforsubject-
initialdeclarativesthanGermandoes,andthatGermanhasagreaterpreference
forobject-initialandadverbial-initialdeclarativesthanSwedishdoes.Thesubtle
differencesgoevenfurtherthanthis,though.Forexample,thetwolanguagesdif-
ferinthetypesofadverbialsthatareconventionallyfronted,withSwedishtend-
ingtoward“phonologicallylight”adverbials(e.g.,så=‘so’or‘then’)andGerman
tendingtowardmorecomplexconstructionsintheprefield.Swedishalsoshows
agreatertendencytowardkeeping“informationallynew(i.e.,rhematicorfocal)
material out of the clause-initial position” (p. 518), whereas German does not
holdastightlytothisprincipleofinformationorganization.Inanexamination
oforalandwrittenL2GermandataproducedbySwedish-speaking learnersat
varyinglevelsofL2proficiency,theresearchersfoundthatthesubtlebutcomplex
webofsimilaritiesanddifferencesbetweenSwedishandGermanregardingthe
typesofinformationthatcanoccurintheprefieldofdeclarativeclausesinboth
languagesaccountsquitewell for theresults.EventhoughtheV2grammatical
constraint inboth languagesgaveevenbeginning learnersanautomaticability
toproducegrammaticalV2constructions inthetarget language, theirreliance
onSwedish-basedconventionsofinformationorganizationresultedinproblems
withidiomaticityandcohesiveness(i.e.,fromanativeGermanspeaker’sperspec-
tive).Moreover,thesesubtlebutimportantCLIeffectspersistedevenathighlev-
elsofL2proficiency(p.534).

When introducing the original framework in my 2000 paper, I illustrated
howallthreeoftheoriginaltypesofevidencecouldbefoundinmydatabaseof
writtensilent-filmretellscollectedfromFinnish-speakingandSwedish-speaking
learnersofEnglish.Thosesamedatacanalsobeusedtoillustrateallfourtypes
ofevidence,includingintralingualcontrasts.Iwillofferabriefexampleinvolving
the use of prepositions by Finnish-speaking and Swedish-speaking learners of
English.Swedishhasprepositionsthatareformallyandfunctionallyquitesimilar
tothoseofEnglish,butFinnishdiffersinseveralimportantrespects.Finnishdoes
haveafewprepositions,suchaslähellä(=‘near’),butmostofthemeaningsthat
areexpressedwithprepositionsinEnglishareexpressedinFinnishwitheither
postpositions(e.g.,puun alla=‘undera/thetree’)orlocativecasesuffixes(e.g.,
talossa=‘inthehouse’).Tobesure,Finnishpostpositionsandnominalcasesuf-
fixesarebothincongruentwithEnglish,butindifferentways.Wecantherefore
examineintralingualcontrastswithrespecttowhetherFinnish-speakinglearners
ofEnglishwillshowdifferencesintheiracquisitionanduseofprepositionswhose
L1counterpartsareprepositions,postpositions,orcasesuffixesinL1Finnish.If

typesofevidence.Whenitisnotfeasibletocollectdatafromspeakersrepresent-
ing more than a single source-language background, for example, intralingual
contrastscanserveessentially tosimulate intergroupheterogeneity.Recall that
intergroupheterogeneityisverifiedthroughabetween-groupcomparisonwhere
theresearchercomparestheuseofasingletargetlanguagebyspeakersrepresent-
ingdifferentsourcelanguages,whichIwillrefertoasL1AandL1B.BecauseL1A
andL1BdifferintermsofhowtheirstructurescorrespondwiththoseoftheL2,
wecanrefertothistypeofcomparisonasL2AversusL2B,or,moreappropriately,
asILAversusILB,whereILmeansinterlanguageorthelearners’ownversionof
thetargetlanguage.Itturnsoutthatinterlingualcontrastsinvolveaverysimilar
type of comparison. That is, the researcher divides the structures of the target
languageintoseparatecategoriesdependingonhowtheycorrespondwithstruc-
turesofthesourcelanguage.Thisessentiallyinvolvessplittingthetargetlanguage
intoanILAversusanILB(ILC,etc.),butinrelationtoasinglesourcebackground
ratherthantwoormoresource-languagebackgrounds.AlthoughIcontinueto
recommendtheuseofasmanytypesofevidenceaspossible,Idoacknowledge
that a within-language comparison involving intralingual contrasts of multiple
structuresisprobablyanappropriatesubstituteforabetween-groupcomparison
whenthelatterisnotfeasible.Eachoftheothertypesofevidenceshouldnormally

stillbeexaminedforpurposesofempiricalrigor,however,evenifoneofthetypes
ofevidencecansuccessfullybesubstitutedbyanother.
Afinal interestingpoint about intralingual contrasts is that theymayhelp
solveasecondweaknessofmymethodologicalframework,whichisthatithas
beenarticulatedmainlyinrelationtoL1influencewithoutofferingaclearindica-
tionofhow,forexample,wecanmethodologicallydifferentiateL1influencefrom
L2influenceinthecaseofthirdlanguageacquisition.Intralingualcontrastscould
beusedtodifferentiatebetweenL1andL2influence,forexample,byclassifying
featuresoftheL3inaccordancewithhowtheycorrespondtofeaturesoftheL1
versustheL2,andbylookingathowtheL3learnersperforminrelationtoeach
ofthesecategories(cf.Dewaele1998,OdlinandJarvis2004).
The detection-based approach
The detection-based argument has its own premises that differ from the four
typesofevidencethatareusedaspremisesforthecomparison-basedargument.
Themainpremiseforthedetection-basedargumentistheaccuracywithwhich
learners’ source-language backgrounds can be detected on the basis of their
target-languageperformance.Asmentionedearlier,thisisaphenomenonthatis
anecdotallyfamiliartoexperiencedlanguageteachersofstudentsfrommultiple
native-languagebackgrounds,andmayalsobefamiliartothereader.Thesim-
plestexamplesofthisphenomenoninvolvecaseswhereapersonhearsorreads
asampleoflanguageproducedbyanunknownperson,andhasastronghunch
ofwhatthenativelanguageofthatpersonis.Ahunchisnotscientificallyvalid,
ofcourse,butwhenhunchesareappliedtomultiplelanguagesamples,andwhen
a person is able to detect multiple learners’ L1s at a rate that significantly and
substantiallyexceedsthelevelofchance,thenthosehunchesbecomeamatterof
scientificinquirybecauseitcanbeassumedinsuchcasesthattherearediscover-
able source-language-specificpatterns in thedataeven if thepersondoing the
identificationisnotconsciouslyawareofwhatthosepatternsmightbe.
Theabilitytodetectforeignaccentsisofcoursewellknown(cf.Flege1984,
Major2007,MunroandDerwing1995),butmuchlessisknownabouthowac-
curatelypeople can identify the specificoriginsof thoseaccents, and it is also
unclearhowwellhumanjudgescandetectlearners’andbilinguals’L1sontheba-
sisofotherfactors,suchaswordchoice,syntax,anddiscoursestyle(cf.Scarcella
1992).Thereare,however,at least twostudies thathaveexaminedthis inrela-
tiontosyntacticcues.Ioup(1984),forexample,lookedatwhetherhumanjudges
couldidentifythenativelanguageofananonymouswriterorspeakeronthebasis
oftheirsyntacticerrors.Sheconcludedthattheycouldnot,butafollow-upstudy
184 ScottJarvis
byOdlin(1996)foundthatSpanish-EnglishandKorean-Englishbilingualswere
indeedcapableofdetectingtheirownL1sonthebasisofsyntacticerrorswhen
presentedwithlanguagesamplesproducedbylearnersfromseveraldifferentL1
backgrounds.Moreresearchinvolvingthehumandetectionofsource-language
backgroundsisclearlycalledfor,notonlyinrelationtolearners’syntacticpat-
terns,butalsoinrelationtotheirphonological,morphological,lexical,anddis-
cursivepatternsofperformance.
Inthemeantime,workhasalreadybegunonmachinedetectionofsource-
languagebackgrounds,andthisworkislinkedtoframeworksreferredtoassignal
detection,patterndetection,machinelearning,andartificialintelligence(Santini
2004, Sebastiani 2002, Stamatatos 2009, Stamatatos, Fakotakis and Kokkinakis
2000).Theseareasofinvestigationusuallyrelyontheautomated,computerized
detectionofbackgroundcharacteristicsoflanguagesamplesratherthanrelying
onhumanjudges,andthisisdoneforreasonsofobjectivityandefficiency.The
assumptionisthatifacomputercanlearntoclassifytextswithrespecttoapar-
ticularcharacteristic,suchasL1background,withhighlevelsofaccuracy,then
thedatamustindeedcontainsometracesofthatcharacteristicevenifthosetraces
arenotobvioustothehumanobserver.
Thefieldsofresearchthatdealwiththistypeofquestionincludecomputer
science,artificialintelligence,andinformationscience,andparticularlythesub-
fieldswithinartificialintelligenceandinformationsciencethatarereferredto,
respectively,asmachinelearningandautomateddocumentclassification(orau-
tomatictextcategorization,whichissometimesseenasabranchofstylometry)
(e.g.,Stamatatosetal.2000).Researchers in theseareas typicallyuseaspecial
computerprogramcalledaclassifierthattakeswhateverinformationisfedinto
itandsearchesforpatternsthatwillallowittolearntopredictwhichcategorya
particulartextbelongsto.Theinformationthatisfedintotheclassifierisusually
lexical,oftenintheformofthefrequenciesoftheindividualwordsthatoccurin
eachtext,butsometimesthevariablesfedintotheclassifierrepresentfrequen-
cies forparticularcombinationsofwords,partsof speech,andevenpunctua-
tion(e.g.,Santini2004).Othervariables, suchas the frequenciesofparticular
phoneticorgrammatical features,couldofcoursealsobeused.Generally, the
purposeistoclassifytextseitherbytexttype(i.e.,characteristicsofthetextit-
self) or author attribute (i.e., characteristics of the person who produced the
text).Studieswiththeformerpurposetendtofocusonthedetectionofthetopic
ofatext,itsgenre,whetheritisaspokenorwrittentext,whetheritisfictionor
non-fiction,orwhetheritisanoriginaltextorhasbeentranslatedfromanother
language.Studieswhosepurposeistoclassifytextsbyauthorattributetendto
concentrateonthedetectionofwhether theauthorhadapositiveornegative
opinionofthetopic(i.e.,authorsentiment),whattheauthor’sgenderis,what

theauthor’snationalityis(suchasBritishorAmerican),whethertheauthoris
a native or non-native speaker, and what the author’s specific L1 is (see, e.g.,
BaroniandBernardini2006).It isofcoursethisfinaltypeofdetectionthat is
mostdirectlyrelevanttoCLI.Oftheseveraldozenstudiesthathaveinvolvedau-
tomatedtextclassification,onlyafewhaveattemptedtodetectL1backgrounds
insamplesofL2speechorwriting.
ThefirstisastudybyMayfieldTomokiyoandJones(2001).Thepurposeof
this study was mainly to determine whether transcriptions of English spoken
textsproducedbynativespeakerscouldbedistinguishedfromthoseproduced
bynon-nativespeakers.However,theresearchersalsoincludedatestofwhether
EnglishtextsproducedbyChinesespeakersandJapanesespeakerscouldbedis-
tinguished from one another. For classification purposes, the researchers used
anaïveBayesclassifier,whichbuildsamodelofprobabilitiesthatassociatetext
features(e.g.,words,partsofspeech)withcategories(e.g.,nativeversusnonna-
tive,ChineseversusJapanese).Thetextfeaturesthatwerefedintotheclassifier
werethefrequenciesofspecifictrigrams(i.e.,three-wordcombinations)foundin
eachtext,aswellasthefrequenciesofthebigramsandunigramsthatthosetri-
gramscontained.Insomeanalyses,theresearchersalsousedfrequencyinforma-
tiononparts-of-speechcategories(e.g.,nouns,verbs)occurringineachtext.The
resultsofthevariousanalysesshowedthattheclassifier’saccuracyindetectingthe
learners’L1srangedfrom80%to100%,dependingonthespecificwords,word
combinations,andparts-of-speechinformationthatwerefedintotheclassifier.
TheimportantpointfromtheperspectiveofCLIisthatthelevelofchancefor
categorizingnonnative textsasbeingwrittenbyChinese speakersversus Japa-
nesespeakersisonly50%(i.e.,50%accuracyofguessingcorrectlywhenthereare
only twochoices),yeteveryanalysis theresearchersranachievedclassification
accuracysubstantiallyhigherthanthis.Theauthorsdidnotconductaqualitative
analysis of the data to determine what the language-specific tendencies of the
ChineseversusJapanesespeakerswere,buttheirhighL1classificationaccuracy
makestheargumentforthepresenceoftransferrelativelysolid,eventhoughthe
context for the argument (i.e., the explanation of what the specific CLI effects
were)remainsweak.
Classificationaccuracybecomesincreasinglychallengingthemorecatego-
ries (e.g., L1s) there are to choose from, so the detection-based evidence for
transferbecomeseven stronger ifhigh levelsof classificationaccuracycanbe
maintainedevenwhentheclassifierhasmultipleL1stochoosefrom.Fromthis
perspective,astudybyKoppel,SchlerandZigdon(2005)isparticularlyinterest-
ing.ThisstudytestedwhetheraclassifiercoulddetecttheL1backgroundsofL2
English essays written by native speakers of the following five L1s: Bulgarian,
Czech,French,Russian,andSpanish.Thedata included258 texts foreachL1

background(i.e.,1290textsintotal),whichweretakenfromtheInternational
CorpusofLearnerEnglish.Each textwasrepresentedasa1035-unitnumeri-
calvector,beingmadeupofrelativefrequenciesofoccurrencefor400standard
functionwords,200specific lettercombinations,185specificerror types,and
250rareparts-of-speechcombinations.Theresearchersusedaclassifierreferred
toassupportvectormachines(SVM),whichcreatesastatisticalmodeltodis-
tinguishvectors(i.e.,texts)belongingtodifferentclasses(i.e.,L1s).Thecross-
validatedresults showed that,withall1035variables in themodel, theclassi-
fierwasabletodetectthelearners’L1swith80.2%accuracy.Thisisaprofound
findinggiventhatthelevelofchancewithfiveL1sisonly20%.Theresearchers
alsoranseparateanalysestodeterminetherelativecontributionsoflexicalfre-
quencies,lettercombinations,anderrortypes.Theseresultsshowedthatlexical
informationaloneresultedinaclassificationaccuracyofroughly75%.Finally,
theresearchersalsoprovidedqualitativeinformationaboutsomeofthedistin-
guishingcharacteristicsofeachL1group.Forexample,theyshowedthatBulgar-
ianwritershadauniquelyhightendencytousethemost-ADVERBcombination,
Frenchwritershadauniquelyhightendencytouseindeed,andRussianwriters
weremorepronethantheothergroupstouseover.
SomeoftheL1sintheKoppeletal.(2005)studywerefairlycloselyrelated
to one another (i.e., Bulgarian, Czech, and Russian; French and Spanish), and
onequestionthatthisraisesiswhetherCLIeffectsarestrongenoughandtext-
classificationclassifiersaresensitiveenoughtodistinguishbetweenL1sthatare
evenmoresimilarthanthese,suchasDanishandSwedish,andPortugueseand
Spanish. Another question it raises is whether so many text features are actu-
ally needed to detect L1s reliably, and whether high levels can be achieved on
thebasisofafewdozenindividualwords(orunigrams)alone.Thesequestions
wereaddressedinastudyIconductedwithcolleagueswhoareformerstudentsof
mine.WepresentedpreliminaryresultsattheSecondLanguageResearchForum
in 2004 (Jarvis, Castañeda-Jiménez and Nielsen 2004) and have more recently
expandedouranalysis.Thefinalversionofthestudyisinprocessandwillappear
inaforthcomingeditedvolume(JarvisandCrossleyforthcoming),whichwillin-
cludeanumberofdetection-basedinvestigationsofCLI.Iwilldescribethisstudy
brieflyinthefollowingtwoparagraphs.
The study included 446 foreign-language learners of English from five L1
backgrounds:Danish,Finnish,Portuguese,Spanish,andSwedish.Thedatawere
writtenfilmretellsofasegmentofChaplin’sfilmModernTimes.Weusedtwo
classifiers to detect the learners’ L1 backgrounds. The first was discriminant
analysis,orDA,whichisavailableintheSPSSstatisticalapplication.Thesecond
wasSVM,whichweranthroughtheRstatisticalpackage(seeBaayen2008).The
variables we fed into both classifiers were simply the relative frequencies with

which53ofthemostfrequentwordsinourdatabasewerefoundtooccurineach
text.Ourchoiceof53wordswasbasedonthesuccessthatauthorshipattribu-
tion studies have had with approximately the same number of high-frequency
words(Burrows2003,Stamatatos,FakotakisandKokkinakis2001).Weoriginally
selectedjustthe50mostfrequentwordsinourdatabase,butwealsowantedto
makesurethatthemostfrequent30wordsfromeachL1groupwereincluded,
andthisresultedinatotalof53words.
Bothclassifiersincludedbothatrainingphaseandacross-validationphase.
Inthetrainingphase,theclassifierswerefednotonlythetextfeatures(i.e.,word
frequencies),butalsotheL1categoryforeachtext.Duringthisphase,theclassi-
fiersadjustedtheirvectorsinordertocreateastatisticalmodelthatmaximized
within-group similarities and between-group differences. Then, the classifiers
proceededtoacross-validationphasewheretheyusedtheirstatisticalmodelsto
predicttheL1sofeachtextwithoutaccesstothetexts’L1categories.Thecross-
validationresultsofbothourDAandSVManalysesshowedthattheclassifiers
wereabletodetectthecorrectL1sofapproximately80%ofthe446textsinour
database(80.3%forDA,and79.6%forSVM),andtheaccuracywashigheven
between the closely related L1s. These results show that important CLI effects
canbefoundinlearners’useofjustafewdozenhighlyfrequentwords,andthat
theseeffectsaredetectableeveninthecontextofcloselyrelatedL1s.Afollow-up
qualitativeanalysisshowedsomeofthedistinguishingcharacteristicsofeachL1
group,suchastheDanishwriters’highuseofintoandout,theFinnishwriters’
lowuseofarticlesandbe,thePortuguesewriters’highuseofforandlowuseof
go,theSpanishwriters’highuseofofandthat,andtheSwedishwriters’highuse
ofwhoandwhen.Together,thecombinationofclassificationaccuracyandquali-
tativeevidenceofspecificCLIeffectsseemtomakeforastrongdetection-based
argumentforCLI,andalsomakethedetection-basedapproachausefulmeans
forconductingexploratoryworkonCLI.
Summary and concluding thoughts
Thepurposeofthispaperhasbeentoproposesomenecessaryrefinementstomy
earlier methodological framework for investigating CLI. These refinements in-
cludeatwo-parameterclassificationschemethatclarifiestherelationshipsamong
thecomparison-basedtypesofevidencefortransferandrecognizesafourthtype
ofevidencenottakenintoaccountintheoriginalversionoftheframework.The
refinements to the framework also include a recognition that the comparison-
basedargumentfortransferco-existswithothervalidargumentsfortransferthat
aregroundedinothertypesofevidence-basedpremises.Ihavehighlightedand

illustratedboththecomparison-anddetection-basedargumentstotransfer,and
havereviewedrelevantrecentstudiesthathavefollowedeachapproach.Asum-
maryof these twoargumentsandthe typesofevidence(orpremises) theyare
basedonisshowninTable5.Here,inadditiontotheprimarytypesofevidence
foreachargumentthatIhaveemphasizedthroughoutthepaper,Ialsoindicate
thatnon-primarypremisescanstrengtheneachargumentbyprovidingrelevant
empirical and/or non-empirical contextualization of the relationships between
thesourceandtarget languages, thetendenciesofnativespeakersofboth,and
the sources,mechanisms,processes,andoutcomesofCLI in specificdomains.

for cross-languagecongruity in thecomparison-basedapproach involves simi-
larities between source-language and target-language patterns of performance,
and this does not yet have a practical counterpart in the detection-based ap-
proach,althoughIbelievethat iteventuallywillascomputerclassifiersarede-
velopedwithgreatersophisticationandforthespecificpurposesofCLIresearch.
Finally, evidence for intralingual contrasts in the comparison-based approach
involveslanguage-specificpatternsofperformanceacrossseveralfeaturesofthe
target languagethatvary intermsofhowtheycorrespondwithfeaturesof the
source language. In the comparison-based approach, the configuration of cor-
respondencesbetweenthesourceandtarget languages isusuallymadeexplicit
throughacontrastiveanalysis(whichmaybeeitherempirical[e.g.,corpus-based]
or non-empirical [e.g., introspective or informally observational/descriptive]).
Thedetection-basedapproach,on theotherhand,usuallydoesnotbeginwith
a contrastive analysis, but instead follows the assumption that some transfer-
relatedpatternsmaybesosubtleastoeludecontrastiveanalysisandothertypes
ofobservation-basedanalysis.Crucially,however,thedetection-basedapproach
doesrelyonmultiplefeaturesofthetargetlanguageandassumesthat,ifthereare
anyCLIeffectsinthedata,theywillbefoundifenoughoftherelevantfeaturesof
thetargetlanguagearefedintotheclassifier.Thus,bothapproachesrelyonmul-
tiplefeaturesofthetargetlanguage(cf.intralingualcontrasts)inordertoidentify
source-language-specificpatternsofL2use.
Fromthisperspective,thedetection-basedapproachisgroundedinthesame
fundamentalgroup-relatedand language-relatedpatterns that thecomparison-
basedapproachis,butitisinterestingisthattheargumentsthemselvesrelyon
different primary premises. In the future, I look forward to working with col-
leagues in the field to develop this framework further with clearer criteria for
investigatingCLIeffectsinmorecomplexcasesofmultilingualism,andalsoin
creatingabetterdevelopedclassificationschemeforallpossiblearguments for
CLI.Asaconcludingthought,Ibeganthispaperwiththeassertionthatinstances
oftransfercanbebothremarkablysimpleandatthesametimeextraordinarily
complex.Whatdetermineswhethertheyaresimpleorcomplexishowtheyare
viewed,andthedepthtowhichtheyareinvestigatedandanalysed.IhopeIhave
beensuccessfulindemonstratingthis,andinshowingthatcertaingeneralprin-
ciplesseemtounderlietransferatalllevelsofcomplexity.
